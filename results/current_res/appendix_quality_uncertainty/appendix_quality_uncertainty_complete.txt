\subsection{Impact of Accounting Uncertainty on Firm Quality}
\label{app:quality_uncertainty_impact}

This appendix documents how the accounting-uncertainty framework changes the firm-level quality signals used in the portfolio analysis. The purpose is descriptive. Rather than evaluating portfolio performance, the appendix shows how the adjustment changes the level, spread, and portfolio ranking of firm quality. The analysis is based on 7,146 firm-year observations for 614 firms over the formation years 2010--2025.

Observed Quality is the unadjusted operating profitability signal. Latent Quality is the empirical Bayes posterior mean, which shrinks uncertain observations toward the annual cross-sectional mean. Conservative Quality applies a direct downward penalty based on accounting uncertainty. Probabilistic Quality does not adjust the level of the signal directly, but instead uses the implied distribution of firm quality to assign firms to the high- and low-quality tails.

\subsubsection{Quality Levels and Quality Spread}

\autoref{fig:quality_levels_mean_median} compares annual mean and median quality before and after adjustment. Latent Quality remains close to Observed Quality because the empirical Bayes adjustment is centered on the cross-sectional mean. Conservative Quality is lower by construction, since the method applies a downward penalty based on uncertainty. The difference is particularly visible in the median series, where Conservative Quality lies below the observed and latent signals in most years.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/quality_levels_mean_median.png}
    \caption[Quality before and after accounting-uncertainty adjustment]{Annual mean and median quality before and after accounting-uncertainty adjustment.}
    \label{fig:quality_levels_mean_median}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:quality_spread_before_after} shows how the adjustment affects the cross-sectional spread of the quality signal. Latent Quality reduces the average annual standard deviation from 1.57 for Observed Quality to 1.38, consistent with shrinkage compressing the cross-section. Conservative Quality, by contrast, preserves more of the original spread. Its average annual standard deviation is 1.58, close to the observed signal. The 5th--95th percentile spread shows the same pattern: Latent Quality narrows the distribution, while Conservative Quality leaves the tail spread broadly intact.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/quality_spread_before_after.png}
    \caption[Quality spread before and after adjustment]{Cross-sectional quality spread before and after adjustment. Panel A reports the annual standard deviation, while Panel B reports the 5th--95th percentile spread.}
    \label{fig:quality_spread_before_after}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Firm-Level Adjustments}

\autoref{fig:quality_adjustment_distribution} shows the distribution of firm-year quality adjustments. The median absolute adjustment is small for Latent Quality, at 0.009, because most firms have relatively precise signals and therefore receive limited shrinkage. The average absolute Latent Quality adjustment is larger, at 0.049, reflecting a smaller number of firm-years with much higher uncertainty. Conservative Quality produces a median absolute adjustment of 0.026 and an average absolute adjustment of 0.041. Since Conservative Quality always applies a downward penalty, its signed adjustment is mechanically negative.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/quality_adjustment_distribution.png}
    \caption[Distribution of firm-year quality adjustments]{Distribution of firm-year quality adjustments. Panel A reports signed changes relative to Observed Quality, while Panel B reports absolute changes.}
    \label{fig:quality_adjustment_distribution}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:observed_vs_adjusted_quality} compares observed quality with the adjusted signals directly. Points close to the 45-degree line are firms whose quality ranking is largely unchanged. Larger deviations from the line occur mainly among firms with higher accounting uncertainty relative to the annual median. The figure also illustrates the difference between the two adjustment methods. Latent Quality pulls observations toward the cross-sectional mean, while Conservative Quality shifts firms downward without symmetric upward adjustment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/observed_vs_adjusted_quality.png}
    \caption[Observed quality versus adjusted quality]{Observed Quality compared with Latent Quality and Conservative Quality. Points are shaded by firm-year accounting uncertainty relative to the annual median.}
    \label{fig:observed_vs_adjusted_quality}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:adjustment_by_sigma_decile} shows that adjustment strength is concentrated among the most uncertain firms. The average uncertainty weight, $1-\lambda_i$, is 0.6\% in the lowest $\sigma_i$ decile, 5.7\% in the fifth decile, and 36.1\% in the highest decile. The mean absolute Latent Quality adjustment increases from 0.001 in the lowest decile to 0.187 in the highest decile. For Conservative Quality, the corresponding increase is from 0.003 to 0.144. The adjustment is therefore not applied evenly across firms, but is concentrated where the accounting signal is estimated to be least precise.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/adjustment_by_sigma_decile.png}
    \caption[Adjustment strength by accounting-uncertainty decile]{Adjustment strength by accounting-uncertainty decile. Panel A reports mean absolute quality adjustments, while Panel B reports the shrinkage weight $\lambda_i$ and the uncertainty weight $1-\lambda_i$.}
    \label{fig:adjustment_by_sigma_decile}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:latent_quality_interval_width} reports the annual width of the approximate 95\% interval around Latent Quality. The average interval width is 1.80, while the median is 0.86, again indicating that uncertainty is unevenly distributed across firms. The wide upper tail is consistent with the adjustment being economically relevant for a subset of firms even though the median firm receives a relatively modest adjustment.

\begin{figure}[H]

    \label{fig:latent_quality_interval_width}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Variation Across Exchanges and Sectors}

\autoref{fig:quality_uncertainty_impact_by_exchange} reports how accounting uncertainty and quality adjustments vary across exchanges. Oslo and Copenhagen have the highest average $\sigma_i$, at 0.105 and 0.106, respectively, while Iceland has the lowest average $\sigma_i$ at 0.073. The mean absolute Latent Quality adjustment is largest for Oslo-listed firms and smallest for Icelandic firms. These differences suggest that the accounting-uncertainty adjustment is not only a firm-level transformation, but also reflects differences in the composition of the regional market.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/quality_uncertainty_impact_by_exchange.png}
    \caption[Impact of accounting uncertainty by exchange]{Impact of accounting uncertainty by exchange. Panel A reports mean absolute quality adjustments, while Panel B reports mean accounting uncertainty.}
    \label{fig:quality_uncertainty_impact_by_exchange}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:quality_uncertainty_impact_by_sector} reports the corresponding sector-level variation. Health Care and Information Technology have the largest average absolute Latent Quality adjustments, with mean absolute adjustments of 0.100 and 0.082, respectively. These sectors also have relatively high mean accounting uncertainty. The sector pattern is consistent with the idea that accounting uncertainty is partly related to business models, operating volatility, and the mapping between accruals and cash-flow fundamentals.

\subsubsection{Distribution and Persistence of Accounting Uncertainty}

\autoref{fig:sigma_evolution} reports the time-series evolution of firm-level accounting uncertainty. Across all firm-years, the mean $\sigma_i$ is 0.101 and the median is 0.084. The mean is above the median, reflecting a right-skewed distribution in which a subset of firms has substantially higher estimated accounting uncertainty. The shaded 5th--95th percentile range confirms that cross-sectional dispersion in accounting uncertainty is persistent throughout the sample.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/sigma_evolution.png}
    \caption[Evolution of accounting uncertainty]{Evolution of accounting uncertainty. The figure reports the annual mean, median, and 5th--95th percentile range of firm-level $\sigma_i$.}
    \label{fig:sigma_evolution}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:sigma_distribution} shows the pooled and annual distributions of $\sigma_i$. The pooled distribution is right-skewed, while the annual box plots show that accounting uncertainty differs substantially across firms within each formation year. This dispersion is important for the portfolio construction framework, because firms with higher $\sigma_i$ receive stronger shrinkage or larger uncertainty penalties.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/sigma_distribution.png}
    \caption[Distribution of accounting uncertainty]{Distribution of accounting uncertainty. Panel A reports the pooled firm-year distribution, while Panel B reports the annual distribution by formation year.}
    \label{fig:sigma_distribution}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:sigma_persistence} evaluates whether accounting uncertainty is persistent at the firm level. The one-year Pearson correlation in $\sigma_i$ is 0.951 and the one-year Spearman correlation is 0.940. Persistence remains meaningful at longer horizons, with Pearson correlations of 0.873 at two years and 0.577 at five years. This supports the interpretation of $\sigma_i$ as a firm-level precision proxy rather than a purely transitory noise measure.

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Portfolio Reclassification}

\autoref{fig:portfolio_transition_heatmaps} shows how Latent Quality and Conservative Quality move firms across quality portfolios relative to Observed Quality. Latent Quality leaves 7.2\% of firm-year portfolio assignments changed across all quintiles, while Conservative Quality changes 15.4\%. In the tails, Latent Quality moves 5.0\% of observed Q1 firm-years and 5.4\% of observed Q5 firm-years out of their original tail portfolios. Conservative Quality moves 8.6\% of observed Q1 firm-years and 6.9\% of observed Q5 firm-years. Most movements are to adjacent portfolios, indicating that the uncertainty adjustment refines rather than completely reshuffles the quality ranking.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/portfolio_transition_heatmaps.png}
    \caption[Portfolio transitions after uncertainty adjustment]{Portfolio transitions after uncertainty adjustment. Rows show portfolios under Observed Quality and columns show portfolios under the adjusted quality signal.}
    \label{fig:portfolio_transition_heatmaps}
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:probabilistic_tail_probabilities} describes the tail probabilities used by Probabilistic Quality. The mean values of $P(Q5)$ and $P(Q1)$ are 0.305 and 0.309, respectively, while the medians are 0.263 and 0.274. Around 20\% of firm-years have probabilities above 0.5 for each tail, as expected from a quintile-based construction, but only 7.5\% to 7.8\% have probabilities above 0.8. This shows that Probabilistic Quality assigns firms to the tails only when the probability of belonging there is sufficiently high, rather than relying only on the point estimate of observed profitability.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{Figures/quality_uncertainty_appendix/probabilistic_tail_probabilities.png}
    \caption[Probabilistic Quality tail probabilities]{Probabilistic Quality tail probabilities. Panel A reports the distribution of $P(Q5)$ and $P(Q1)$, while Panel B plots tail probabilities against Observed Quality.}
    \label{fig:probabilistic_tail_probabilities}
\end{figure}

Overall, the figures show that accounting uncertainty affects firm quality in a targeted way. Most firms receive limited adjustment, while firms with high estimated accounting uncertainty receive substantial shrinkage or penalties. Latent Quality compresses the cross-sectional spread of quality, whereas Conservative Quality preserves more of the original spread while lowering firms with uncertain signals. The portfolio transition evidence shows that these changes mainly affect firms near portfolio breakpoints, with larger but still moderate changes in the extreme quality portfolios. This helps explain why the uncertainty-adjusted methods alter portfolio performance only modestly in the main results.
